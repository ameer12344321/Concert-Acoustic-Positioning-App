1. Inverse Square Law

Sound gets weaker as distance increases. For each speaker i at point P:

Li = 1 / ri

where ri is distance from point P to speaker i.

2. Speed of Sound (Temperature Effect)

v = 331.3 + 0.606 × T

T in °C, v in m/s. Warmer air = faster sound = different wavelengths.

3. Wave Interference (Superposition)

Sound waves from multiple speakers add up. At point P:

Atotal = √[(Σ Ai cos φi)² + (Σ Ai sin φi)²]

where φi = 2π × f × ri / v is the phase of wave i at point P.

We average over 3 frequencies (200, 1000, 4000 Hz) for realistic results.

4. Balance Score

Standard deviation of loudness across speakers:

σ = √[(1/N) Σ (Li − L̄)²]

Low σ = all speakers sound equally loud = balanced mix.

5. Best Spot Score

score = Atotal / (1 + σ)

Highest score = loud AND balanced = best spot to stand.

6. Wind Refraction (Outdoor)

Wind bends sound waves by effectively changing the speed of sound along the path:

veffective = v + wind × cos(θ)

where θ is the angle between wind direction and sound travel direction.

7. Safety Consideration

The inverse square law predicts infinitely loud sound as distance approaches zero, but in real life, sound near speakers can cause hearing damage. Concert speakers can reach 120+ dB at 1m, which damages hearing in seconds.

This app applies a 3 meter safety radius around each speaker. Any spot inside this zone is marked as unsafe and excluded from the best spot calculation. This matches real concert practice where delay towers are elevated 4 to 6 meters above the crowd so people can stand nearby safely.

Limitations

This app uses a simplified acoustic model for educational purposes. It does not account for:

  • Sound reflections off walls, ceilings, or the floor
  • Sound absorption by the crowd and materials
  • Speaker dispersion patterns (we assume omnidirectional sources)
  • Frequency-dependent air absorption
  • Vertical sound propagation (we model in 2D)

For professional audio engineering, consult specialized acoustic simulation software. This tool is designed to demonstrate core physics concepts, not to replace industry-standard acoustic modeling.
